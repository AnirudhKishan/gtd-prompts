# GTD — A Psychologically Safe Interpretation

## Core Philosophy

GTD exists to reduce cognitive load, not to maximize system correctness.
The system serves attention and execution, not completeness or rigor.

---

## Non-Negotiable Outcomes

* I trust my system.
* I can choose what to do next with low friction.

Everything else is optional.

---

## Foundational Principles

### 1. Psychological Safety First

Interacting with the system must feel safe.
No interaction should imply:

* global cleanup
* irreversible commitment
* moral obligation

Partial engagement is always allowed.

---

### 2. Shallow Before Deep

Early interaction should be:

* fast
* reversible
* low-semantic

Depth is earned later, when intent exists.

---

### 3. Clarification Is Classification, Not Commitment

Clarifying an item answers:

> “Where does this belong in my attention economy?”

It does **not** answer:

* how to do it
* when to do it
* how important it is in absolute terms

---

### 4. Relevance Before Readiness

An item must first prove it deserves attention.
Only later does it earn execution detail.

Relevance is cheap to decide.
Readiness is expensive and must be deliberate.

---

### 5. Active Projects Are What I Am Driving This Cycle

“Active” means:

> “I am choosing to drive this this cycle.”

It does **not** mean:

* everything I am accountable for
* urgent
* progressing
* must be worked on daily

Projects I am carrying but not driving this cycle live elsewhere — in **Overflow** (wants Active, blocked by the cap), **Waiting-For** (blocked on external input), or **Maybe** (not on radar this cycle).

Active projects are explicitly WIP-limited (≤ 5).

---

### 6. Next Actions Exist to Enable Doing

Next actions are tools for execution.
They are not admission tickets.

A next action is decided:

* when I intend to act
* when energy is available
* when the decision will likely survive reality

---

### 7. No Global Correctness Assumption

Touching one item does not obligate fixing the system elsewhere.
Local actions stay local.

The system is allowed to be:

* messy
* inconsistent
* temporarily wrong

---

### 8. Ambiguity Is a Valid State

Not everything needs resolution.
Vague, parked, or dormant items are acceptable.

Clarity is pulled when needed, not pushed prematurely.

---

### 9. WIP Limits Create Trust

Reducing the active surface area matters more than refining it.
Pruning precedes planning.

A smaller trusted list beats a perfect large one.

---

### 9b. Capacity and Appetite Are Separate Axes

Two distinct questions govern where a project lives:

* **Appetite** — “Do I want to drive this this cycle?”
* **Capacity** — “Is there a slot in Active?”

Conflating them is what causes Active to drift into a wishlist.

* Appetite + capacity → **Active Projects**
* Appetite, no capacity → **Overflow**
* No appetite this cycle → **Maybe**
* Blocked on external → **Waiting-For**

Overflow exists precisely because appetite can legitimately exceed capacity. Denying that forces honest accountability into Maybe, where it doesn't belong.

---

### 10. Exit Ramps Are Mandatory

Every interaction must allow:

* stopping early
* skipping hard items
* leaving things unfinished

Completion is optional.
Safety is required.

---

## Rationales (Why This Works)

* Avoidance is a signal of excessive cognitive cost.
* Over-early structure creates false commitment.
* Humans plan best at the moment of action, not intake.
* Trust emerges from safety, not rigor.

---

## Design Axioms

* If it creates dread, it is misdesigned.
* If clarification feels heavy, it’s doing too much.
* If next actions feel stale, they were created too early.
* If the system resists entry, it has become the problem.

---

## Operational Guardrail: Sequencing Rule

### Active Projects First, Next Actions Later

**Rule:** Next actions may only be created *after* a project has been explicitly added to **Active Projects**.

#### Intake / Clarification Phase

When an item leaves **In**, the only allowed operation is:

> **Clarify → classify → park**

Clarification decides:

* trash / reference / someday
* which project (if any) it belongs to
* whether it is blocked or waiting

Clarification explicitly does **not** include:

* defining how to do the work
* creating next actions
* committing to execution

Items that are not part of an **Active Project** must remain without next actions.

#### Activation Phase

A project becomes eligible for next actions **only when**:

* it is intentionally placed in **Active Projects** (WIP-limited, ≤ 5)
* you are in a review or execution context

Activation typically pulls from **Overflow** (the queue of projects that already want Active and are blocked only by the cap). Occasionally a starred **Maybe** item may activate directly.

At that point, create:

* at most one next action
* concrete and immediately usable

#### Invariant

> **No Active Project → No Next Action**

This guardrail prevents premature structure, false commitment, and next-action bloat.

---

## Guiding Sentence

> The system exists to make starting easy and choosing light — not to make everything defined.
